What This Is

EPL Predictor is a side project that predicts English Premier League match outcomes using machine learning. It is not a betting tool. It does not guarantee results. It is an honest attempt to see how far statistical modelling can go with publicly available football data.

How the Model Works

The pre-match model is an XGBoost gradient-boosted tree classifier trained on 11 EPL seasons (2014–2025). It predicts three outcomes: Home Win, Draw, or Away Win.

26 features are used. We started with 58 candidate features and used Recursive Feature Elimination to find the optimal set — these 26 outperformed larger feature sets in independent testing:

  • Bookmaker implied odds — Bet365 historical odds converted to implied probabilities (home/draw/away, home edge, favourite indicator)
  • Form — last 5 matches with exponential decay weighting (points, goals for/against, wins, draws)
  • Home/away specific form — how each team performs at home vs away
  • League position & points — current standings, goal difference, points gap
  • ELO ratings — historical strength ratings calculated from all past results
  • Pi-ratings — an alternative team strength rating optimised for football prediction
  • Expected Goals (xG) — rolling 5-match average xG created and conceded. Removes luck from shot quality — a team can score 3 lucky long-range goals but their xG tells the real story

Hyperparameters were tuned with Optuna (Bayesian optimisation) and validated with walk-forward cross-validation to prevent lookahead bias.

The model was trained on the first 80% of matches (chronologically) and tested on the remaining 20% it had never seen. Walk-forward validation re-trains on expanding windows to simulate real deployment. Both numbers are honest — no cherry-picking.

Honest Limitations
  • 3.7% draw recall — the model rarely predicts draws. Draws are 23% of EPL outcomes but the model finds it more accurate to predict Home/Away. This remains the single biggest weakness.
  • Promoted teams have less data — teams like Ipswich or Sunderland with only 1–2 EPL seasons in our dataset have fewer training examples. Predictions for these teams are less reliable.
  • No player-level data — the model knows team form and strength but not who is playing. A key injury or tactical change is invisible to it.
  • Football is inherently unpredictable — even with perfect data, match outcomes have a ceiling. The difference between …% and a coin flip is meaningful, but it still means 4 in 10 predictions are wrong.
